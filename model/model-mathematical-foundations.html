Overview

A Mathematical Framework for Modeling Knowledge as a Dynamic System

MLKN.model is the computational epistemology engine at the heart of MLKN.lab. It treats scientific knowledge as a dynamic, multi-layered network and provides the mathematical and algorithmic tools to analyze, simulate, and predict its evolution.


Just as physics engines simulate the behavior of physical systems, MLKN.model simulates the behavior of knowledge systems— revealing hidden patterns, testing hypotheses, and enabling breakthroughs in meta-science, AI, and interdisciplinary research.

Mathematical Foundations

From Multilayer Networks to Polyhierarchical Hypergraphs

Overview Mathematical Foundations Comparison

MLKN.model formalizes knowledge as a polyhierarchical hypergraph with two complementary representations:

  1. A multilayer network for hierarchical structure (Layers 1-4)
  2. A hypergraph for cross-domain connections (Layers 4-5)

This dual approach enables both top-down disciplinary analysis and bottom-up conceptual mapping.

⬢

Multilayer Network Definition (Layers 1-4)

Let

G = (V, E, L, W)
be a directed, weighted multilayer network where:

  • V =
    ∪l=1 to 4 Vl
    is the set of nodes, partitioned into 4 layers:
    • V1
      : Core Domains (e.g., "Formal Sciences")
    • V2
      : Academic Disciplines (e.g., "Computer Science")
    • V3
      : Subdisciplines (e.g., "AI")
    • V4
      : Thematic Domains (e.g., "Machine Learning")
  • E =
    ∪l=1 to 3 El→l+1
    is the set of directed edges between layers:
    • E1→2
      : Core Domains → Disciplines
    • E2→3
      : Disciplines → Subdisciplines
    • E3→4
      : Subdisciplines → Thematic Domains
  • L:
    V → {1, 2, 3, 4}
    is the layer assignment function
  • W:
    E → [0, 1]
    is the weight function (e.g., based on OpenAlex co-occurrence data)

Key Property: G is polyhierarchical - nodes in Layer l can have multiple parents in Layer l-1.
Example: "Cognitive Science" (L2) may belong to both "Psychology" and "Computer Science" (L1).

Directed Weighted Polyhierarchical
⬢

Hypergraph Definition (Layers 4-5)

Let

H = (X, E, w)
be a weighted hypergraph where:

  • X =
    V4 ∪ V5
    is the set of nodes:
    • V4
      : Thematic Domains (shared with multilayer network)
    • V5
      : Concepts (e.g., "Attention Mechanism")
  • E ⊆
    ℙ(X)\{∅}
    is the set of hyperedges, where each hyperedge
    e ∈ E
    connects a subset of nodes
    S ⊆ X
    with
    |S| ≥ 2
  • w:
    E → [0, 1]
    is the weight function

Key Property: Hyperedges can connect any number of nodes across Layers 4-5, enabling representation of:

  • Multi-field concepts (e.g., "Transformers" in CS, Linguistics, and Neuroscience)
  • Non-linear relationships (e.g., "Cognition" spanning Psychology, Philosophy, and AI)

Undirected Multi-Edge Weighted
⬢

Comparison: Multilayer Network vs. Hypergraph

The two representations serve complementary purposes in MLKN.model:

Feature Multilayer Network Hypergraph
Layers 1-4 (Core Domains → Thematic Domains) 4-5 (Thematic Domains → Concepts)
Edges Pairs of nodes (vi, vj) Subsets of nodes S ⊆ X, |S| ≥ 2
Hierarchy Polyhierarchical (multiple parents allowed) Flat (no inherent hierarchy)
Purpose Hierarchical organization Cross-domain connections

Why Both? The multilayer network captures the disciplinary structure of science, while the hypergraph captures the interdisciplinary nature of modern research.

⬢

Unified Polyhierarchical Hypergraph

MLKN.model combines both representations into a polyhierarchical hypergraph

P = (G, H)
where:

  • G = (
    V1-4
    ,
    E1-3
    , L, W) is the multilayer network
  • H = (
    X4-5
    ,
    EH
    , w) is the hypergraph
  • V4 = X4
    (Layer 4 is shared between G and H)

Key Innovation: Layer 4 (Thematic Domains) acts as the bridge between:

  • The hierarchical structure of Layers 1-3 (multilayer network)
  • The cross-domain connections of Layer 5 (hypergraph)

Mathematical Foundations

Formalizing Knowledge as a Dynamic, Multi-Layered Hypergraph

MLKN.model formalizes knowledge as a dynamic, multi-layered hypergraph where:

  • Nodes represent disciplines, subdisciplines, concepts, or themes.
  • Hyperedges represent relationships (hierarchical, interdisciplinary, thematic) between any number of nodes.
  • Layers represent levels of abstraction (e.g., Core Domains → Concepts).
  • Dynamics represent temporal evolution (e.g., emergence of new disciplines).

This formalization enables rigorous analysis, simulation, and prediction of knowledge systems.

⬢

Hypergraph Definition

Let

H = (V, E, L, W, T)
be a multi-layered hypergraph where:

  • V =
    {v1, v2, ..., vn}
    is the set of nodes, where each node
    vi
    represents a discipline, subdiscipline, or concept with attributes:
    • vi.id
      : Unique identifier (e.g., "computer_science").
    • vi.name
      : Human-readable name (e.g., "Computer Science").
    • vi.layer ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}
      : Ontological layer (1=Core Domains, 5=Concepts).
    • vi.domain
      : Parent domain (e.g., "Formal Sciences").
    • vi.metadata
      : Additional attributes (e.g., description, citations, timestamps).
  • E =
    {e1, e2, ..., em}
    is the set of hyperedges, where each hyperedge
    ej
    connects any subset of nodes
    S ⊆ V
    with attributes:
    • ej.nodes
      : Set of nodes connected by the hyperedge (e.g., {"computer_science", "ai", "neuroscience"}).
    • ej.type ∈ {"hierarchical", "interdisciplinary", "thematic"}
      : Type of relationship.
    • ej.weight ∈ [0, 1]
      : Strength of the connection (e.g., derived from co-citation frequency).
    • ej.metadata
      : Additional attributes (e.g., timestamp, source).
  • L:
    V → {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}
    is the layer assignment function, mapping each node to its ontological layer.
  • W:
    E → [0, 1]
    is the weight function, assigning a strength to each hyperedge.
  • T:
    V × E → ℝ+
    is the temporal function, assigning timestamps to nodes/edges (for dynamic analysis).
⬢

Layered Structure (Ontological Hierarchy)

The hypergraph H is organized into 5 ontological layers, forming a polyhierarchy:

  1. Layer 1: Core Domains (e.g., Formal Sciences, Natural Sciences).
    • V1 = {v | L(v) = 1}
    • Represents the highest level of abstraction.
  2. Layer 2: Disciplines (e.g., Computer Science, Psychology).
    • V2 = {v | L(v) = 2}
    • Connected to Layer 1 via hierarchical hyperedges.
  3. Layer 3: Subdisciplines (e.g., AI, Cognitive Psychology).
    • V3 = {v | L(v) = 3}
    • Connected to Layer 2 via hierarchical hyperedges.
  4. Layer 4: Thematic Domains (e.g., Machine Learning, Attention).
    • V4 = {v | L(v) = 4}
    • Connected to Layer 3 via hierarchical hyperedges.
  5. Layer 5: Concepts (e.g., Neural Networks, Memory).
    • V5 = {v | L(v) = 5}
    • Connected to Layer 4 via hierarchical hyperedges.

Polyhierarchy Property: A node in Layer

i
can belong to multiple parent nodes in Layer
i-1
. For example, "AI Ethics" (Layer 3) may belong to both "Computer Science" and "Philosophy" (Layer 2).

⬢

Hypergraph Properties

The hypergraph H exhibits the following properties:

  • Multi-Layered: Nodes are organized into 5 ontological layers (Core Domains → Concepts).
  • Polyhierarchical: Nodes can have multiple parents (e.g., a subdiscipline can belong to multiple disciplines).
  • Weighted: Hyperedges have weights representing connection strength (e.g., co-citation frequency).
  • Directed: Hyperedges can be directed (e.g., hierarchical relationships) or undirected (e.g., thematic connections).
  • Dynamic: Nodes and hyperedges have timestamps to track evolution over time.
  • Attributed: Nodes and hyperedges carry metadata (e.g., descriptions, citations, domains).
⬢

Mathematical Operations

MLKN.model supports the following mathematical operations on the hypergraph H:

  • Centrality Measures:
    • Degree Centrality:
      CD(v) = |{e ∈ E | v ∈ e.nodes}|
      (number of hyperedges connected to v).
    • Betweenness Centrality:
      CB(v) = Σ (σst(v) / σst),
      where σst(v) is the number of shortest paths
      from s to t passing through v.
    • Eigenvector Centrality:
      CE(v) = (1/λ) Σu∈N(v) Auv CE(u),
      where A is the adjacency matrix and
      λ is the largest eigenvalue.
  • Community Detection:
    • Modularity:
      Q = (1/2m) Σij [Aij - (kikj/2m)] δ(ci, cj)
      , where
      m
      is the total weight of hyperedges,
      ki
      is the weighted degree of node
      i
      , and
      ci
      is the community of node
      i
      .
    • Louvain Method: Optimizes modularity via greedy optimization.
    • Leiden Algorithm: Improves Louvain by ensuring communities are well-connected.
  • Path Analysis:
    • Shortest Path:
      d(s, t) = min |P|
      , where
      P
      is a path from
      s
      to
      t
      .
    • Random Walks: Simulate diffusion processes to model knowledge spread.
    • Persistent Homology: Identify topological features (e.g., holes, connected components) in the hypergraph.
  • Dynamic Analysis:
    • Temporal Slices: Analyze the hypergraph at different time points
      Ht = (Vt, Et)
      .
    • Evolution Metrics: Track changes in centrality, modularity, and connectivity over time.
    • Predictive Modeling: Use machine learning to forecast the emergence of new nodes/edges.
⬢

Example: Modeling Knowledge Diffusion

To model how a new concept (e.g., "Transformers") diffuses across disciplines:

  1. Step 1: Represent the Concept

    Add a new node

    vnew = "Transformers"
    to Layer 5 (Concepts) with:
    • vnew.layer = 5
    • vnew.domain = "Machine Learning"
    • vnew.timestamp = 2017
      (year of introduction).

  2. Step 2: Connect to Existing Nodes

    Add hyperedges to connect

    vnew
    to related nodes:
    • e1.nodes = {"Transformers", "Neural Networks", "NLP"}
      (interdisciplinary connection).
    • e1.type = "thematic"
    • e1.weight = 0.95
      (strong connection).

  3. Step 3: Simulate Diffusion

    Use a random walk to simulate how researchers "discover"

    vnew
    :
    • Start at a random node (e.g., "Computer Science").
    • Traverse hyperedges with probability proportional to their weights.
    • Measure the time to reach
      vnew
      from other nodes.

  4. Step 4: Predict Adoption

    Use centrality measures to predict which disciplines will adopt

    vnew
    :
    • Disciplines with high
      CB(v)
      (betweenness centrality) are likely to adopt early.
    • Disciplines in the same community as
      vnew
      (via modularity) are likely to adopt first.

Formal Definitions

Below are the formal definitions of key concepts in MLKN.model:

  • Knowledge Hypergraph:

    A tuple

    H = (V, E, L, W, T)
    where:
    • V
      is the set of nodes (disciplines, concepts, etc.).
    • E
      is the set of hyperedges (relationships).
    • L: V → {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}
      is the layer assignment function.
    • W: E → [0, 1]
      is the weight function.
    • T: V × E → ℝ+
      is the temporal function.

  • Polyhierarchy:

    A hypergraph is polyhierarchical if for some

    v ∈ V
    , there exist
    u, w ∈ V
    such that:
    • L(u) = L(v) - 1
      and
      u
      is a parent of
      v
      .
    • L(w) = L(v) - 1
      and
      w
      is a parent of
      v
      .
    • u ≠ w
      .
    In other words, a node can have multiple parents in the layer above it.

  • Knowledge Diffusion:

    A stochastic process on

    H
    where a random walker traverses hyperedges with probability:
    • P(v → u | e) = W(e) / Σe' ∋ v W(e')
      , where
      e
      is a hyperedge containing
      v
      and
      u
      .

Why This Hybrid Approach Matters

Traditional knowledge representations suffer from trade-offs:

Hierarchies (e.g., Taxonomies)

  • ✅ Clear parent-child relationships
  • ✅ Intuitive for domain experts
  • ❌ Cannot model multi-field concepts
  • ❌ Rigid structure

Hypergraphs (e.g., Knowledge Graphs)

  • ✅ Captures complex relationships
  • ✅ Flexible for cross-domain concepts
  • ❌ No inherent hierarchy
  • ❌ Harder to navigate for non-experts

MLKN.model's polyhierarchical hypergraph combines the strengths of both: hierarchical clarity + cross-domain flexibility.
